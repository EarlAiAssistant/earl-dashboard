## Introduction: Why Customer Interview Content Works

Every week, your company has conversations with customers:
- Onboarding calls
- Success check-ins
- Support escalations
- Quarterly business reviews
- Renewal conversations

**Every one of those calls is a content opportunity.**

But most companies treat these conversations as one-and-done interactions. They happen, maybe someone takes notes, and then they're forgotten.

**What if you captured those conversations and turned them into:**
- ✅ Case studies (your highest-converting content)
- ✅ Blog posts (SEO traffic magnets)
- ✅ Testimonials (social proof that converts)
- ✅ Social media content (authenticity that resonates)
- ✅ Sales enablement material (real objection handling)
- ✅ Product feedback (what to build next)

**That's customer interview content marketing.**

---

### **The Problem with Traditional Content Marketing**

**Traditional approach:**
1. Brainstorm blog topics
2. Research keywords
3. Write from scratch
4. Hope it ranks and converts

**Time required:** 4-6 hours per blog post  
**Success rate:** Low (most content gets < 100 views)

---

**Customer interview approach:**
1. Record a customer conversation you're already having
2. Transcribe it (5 minutes, automated)
3. Extract quotes and structure (30 minutes)
4. Polish and publish (30 minutes)

**Time required:** 1-2 hours (80% faster)  
**Success rate:** High (real stories, real SEO value, real conversions)

---

### **Why This Works**

**1. People Trust Customers, Not Companies**

**Your claim:** "Our software increases productivity by 50%."  
**Reaction:** "Sure, everyone says that."

**Customer quote:** "Before this, I was working 60-hour weeks. Now I work 40 and get more done. This literally gave me my weekends back."  
**Reaction:** "Wait, really? Tell me more."

---

**2. Google Rewards Original, Helpful Content**

Generic "10 Tips for Better Productivity" posts are a dime a dozen.

But "How Sarah Cut Her Workweek From 60 Hours to 40 Hours Using [Specific Tool]" is:
- Original (unique story)
- Specific (real numbers, real person)
- Helpful (actionable lessons)

Google ranks that higher.

---

**3. You're Creating Content You Were Going to Create Anyway**

The interview was happening whether you recorded it or not. You're just **capturing value that was already there.**

<a name="identify"></a>
## How to Identify Interview-Worthy Customers

Not every customer makes a great case study. Look for:

### **✅ 1. Measurable Results**

**Good candidates have specific, impressive metrics:**
- Time saved: "Cut reporting time from 10 hours to 30 minutes per week"
- Cost saved: "Reduced customer acquisition cost by 40%"
- Revenue increased: "Closed 5 additional deals in the first month"
- Efficiency gained: "Went from 3-day onboarding to 2-hour onboarding"

**Red flags:**
- Vague results ("It's been helpful")
- No measurable change
- Still in early stages (no results yet)

---

### **✅ 2. Clear Before/After Transformation**

**The best stories have a dramatic shift:**

**Before:** Chaos, manual work, frustration, wasted time  
**After:** Automated, organized, happy, productive

**Example:**
- **Before:** Sarah spent 20 hours/week manually entering data, worked until 9 PM every night
- **After:** Automated data entry, leaves work at 5:30 PM, has weekends back

**That's a story people want to read.**

---

### **✅ 3. Represents Your Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)**

Interview customers who look like your target audience.

**If you sell to:**
- **SMB SaaS companies** → Feature SMB SaaS companies (not enterprises)
- **Marketing agencies** → Feature marketing agencies (not consultants)
- **E-commerce brands** → Feature e-commerce brands (not B2B)

**Why this matters:**  
Prospects think, "That customer is just like me. If it worked for them, it'll work for me."

---

### **✅ 4. Good Communicators**

Some customers are naturally better storytellers than others.

**Look for customers who:**
- Are enthusiastic when they talk about your product
- Provide detailed answers (not just "yeah, it's good")
- Can articulate problems and solutions clearly
- Are comfortable on video/audio calls

**Where to find them:**
- High NPS scores (9-10 promoters)
- Active in community forums or support channels
- Have left detailed positive reviews
- Your Customer Success team's favorite customers

---

### **✅ 5. Willing to Be Public**

Some customers love being featured. Others can't (employer restrictions, privacy concerns).

**Ask:**
- "Would you be comfortable being named in a case study?"
- "Can we use your company name and logo?"
- "Would you be willing to share this on your LinkedIn?"

**If they say no to all three:** You can still do an anonymous case study, but it's less powerful.

---

### **How to Find These Customers**

**Method 1: NPS Surveys**

Send a simple survey:
> "On a scale of 0-10, how likely are you to recommend [Product] to a colleague?"

**9-10 respondents = interview candidates.**

Follow up:
> "Thanks for the 10! Would you be up for a quick call to share your story? We'd love to feature your success."

---

**Method 2: Usage Data**

Look at your product analytics:
- **Power users** (high engagement, using advanced features)
- **Long-term customers** (12+ months, still active)
- **Teams that expanded** (added users, upgraded plans)

---

**Method 3: Ask Your CS Team**

Your Customer Success team knows who loves your product.

Ask:
> "Who are your 5 favorite customers? Who's had the best results?"

---

**Method 4: Monitor Support Tickets**

Customers who had a problem, got it resolved, and are now happy make **great stories.**

**Example:**
"We had a major issue with [Feature]. The support team fixed it in 24 hours, and now we're seeing 3x better results than before."

That's a case study about both your product *and* your customer service.

**How to ask:**

> "Hey, do you mind if I record this call? I like to go back and review conversations so I don't miss anything. I'll send you a draft before publishing. Is that okay?"

**99% of people say yes.**

<a name="interview-script"></a>
## The Perfect Interview Script

Use this proven structure:

### **Opening (2 minutes)**

> "Thanks so much for doing this! Before we start, do you mind if I record this for notes? I'll send you a draft before publishing anything."

> "I'm going to ask about your experience with [Product]. Feel free to be totally honest — good or bad. The more specific you can be, the better."

---

### **Section 1: The Before State (5 minutes)**

**Goal:** Understand the pain points and context before they found you.

**Questions:**
1. "What was your situation like before you found us?"
2. "What specific challenges were you facing?"
3. "What had you tried before that didn't work?"
4. "What was the breaking point that made you look for a solution?"
5. "How was this problem affecting your team / business / customers?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Specific pain points (not vague "it was hard")
- Consequences (what was at stake if they didn't solve this?)
- Emotion (how did they *feel* about the problem?)

---

### **Section 2: Discovery and Decision (5 minutes)**

**Goal:** Understand how they found you and why they chose you.

**Questions:**
6. "How did you find us?"
7. "What almost stopped you from trying us?"
8. "Why did you choose us over [Competitor A / Competitor B / building it yourself]?"
9. "What made you confident this would work?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Acquisition channel (how they discovered you)
- Objections (and how they overcame them)
- Differentiation (why you beat competitors)

---

### **Section 3: Implementation (5 minutes)**

**Goal:** Understand the setup process and early experience.

**Questions:**
10. "Walk me through the first week. What was setup like?"
11. "What surprised you (good or bad)?"
12. "Did you face any challenges getting started? How did you solve them?"
13. "When did you have that 'aha' moment where you thought, 'Okay, this is going to work'?"

**What you're listening for:**
- How easy/hard was it to get started?
- Unexpected challenges (and how to avoid them)
- The moment they knew it would work

---

### **Section 4: Results (7 minutes)**

**Goal:** Capture specific, measurable outcomes.

**Questions:**
14. "What changed for you after you started using [Product]?"
15. "Can you share any specific numbers? Time saved, money saved, revenue increased?"
16. "What's been the biggest win for you personally?"
17. "How has this affected your team / business / customers?"
18. "What's something you didn't expect when you started using this?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Metrics (hard numbers)
- Qualitative wins (peace of mind, team morale)
- Unexpected benefits

---

### **Section 5: Advice and Takeaways (3 minutes)**

**Goal:** Pull out lessons and recommendations.

**Questions:**
19. "If a friend was in the same situation you were in six months ago, what would you tell them?"
20. "What would you have done differently if you could go back?"
21. "If you had to describe your experience in one sentence, what would you say?"

**What you're listening for:**
- Advice for others (makes the content actionable)
- Mistakes to avoid
- One-liner pull quotes

---

### **Wrap-Up (1 minute)**

> "Is there anything I didn't ask that you think is important?"

> "Thanks so much for your time! I'll write this up and send you a draft before we publish anything. If there's anything you want me to change or remove, just let me know."

---

**Total time:** 20-30 minutes

<a name="questions"></a>
## How to Ask Questions That Get Quotable Answers

Great quotes don't happen by accident. You have to ask great questions.

### **❌ Closed Questions (Yes/No)**

**Bad:** "Did you like the product?"  
**Answer:** "Yeah."

**Bad:** "Did it save you time?"  
**Answer:** "Yes."

**Result:** No depth, no quotes, boring.

---

### **✅ Open-Ended Questions**

**Good:** "How has your workflow changed since you started using this?"  
**Answer:** "Oh man, it's night and day. I used to spend 10 hours a week on manual data entry. Now it's automated. I literally got 10 hours of my life back."

**Result:** Specific, emotional, quotable.

---

### **The Best Question Starters**

- **"How..."** (gets you process and details)
- **"What..."** (gets you specifics)
- **"Tell me about..."** (gets you stories)
- **"Walk me through..."** (gets you step-by-step)
- **"Why..."** (gets you motivation and emotion)

---

### **Advanced Tactics**

**1. Dig Deeper**

When someone says something interesting, don't move on. Double-click on it.

**Customer:** "It made things easier."  
**You:** "Tell me more — what do you mean by 'easier'?"  
**Customer:** "Well, I used to stay up until midnight trying to catch up. Now I'm done by 5:30. I actually have time for my family now."

**That's the quote.**

---

**2. Use Silence**

After the customer finishes answering, count to 3 before asking the next question.

**What happens:**  
The customer often keeps talking — and that's where the best stuff comes out.

---

**3. Ask for Examples**

**Customer:** "It saved us a lot of time."  
**You:** "Can you give me a specific example of when that happened?"  
**Customer:** "Last week, we had a big client request. Normally that would've taken me 4 hours to pull the data. This time, I clicked a button and had it in 5 minutes. The client was blown away."

**Now you have a story, not just a claim.**

---

**4. Press for Numbers**

**Customer:** "We saw great results."  
**You:** "Can you ballpark it? Like, 20% improvement? 50%?"  
**Customer:** "Probably closer to 70-80%."

**Numbers make content credible and skimmable.**

<a name="mistakes"></a>
## Common Interview Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

### **Mistake #1: Not Recording**

**Problem:** You frantically type notes and miss half of what they say.

**Fix:** Always record (with permission). Use Zoom, Otter, or Fireflies.

[Read more: The Complete Guide to Recording Customer Calls](#)

---

### **Mistake #2: Talking Too Much**

**Problem:** You spend more time explaining your product than listening to the customer.

**Fix:** 80/20 rule — the customer talks 80%, you talk 20%.

---

### **Mistake #3: Not Digging Deeper**

**Problem:** Customer gives a surface-level answer, and you move on.

**Fix:** When someone says something interesting, follow up with "Tell me more about that" or "Can you give me an example?"

---

### **Mistake #4: Asking Vague Questions**

**Problem:** "How do you feel about the product?" gets you vague answers.

**Fix:** Ask specific questions tied to moments, outcomes, or emotions.

[Read more: How to Get Customers to Say Quotable Things](#)

---

### **Mistake #5: Skipping the Silence**

**Problem:** You fill every pause with another question.

**Fix:** After the customer answers, count to 3 before asking the next question. They'll often keep talking.

---

### **Mistake #6: Not Getting Permission**

**Problem:** You publish content with the customer's name without asking them first. They're unhappy.

**Fix:** Always send a draft and get written approval before publishing.

---

### **Mistake #7: Not Asking for Specifics**

**Problem:** Customer says "a lot" or "better," and you accept it.

**Fix:** Politely press: "When you say 'a lot,' can you ballpark it? 5 hours? 10?"

<a name="legal"></a>
## Legal and Ethical Considerations

### **Recording Consent Laws**

**One-Party Consent States (most U.S. states):**  
You can record as long as you (one party) consent. But you should still tell people.

**Two-Party Consent States:**  
California, Connecticut, Florida, Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, Montana, New Hampshire, Pennsylvania, Washington.  
You *must* get permission before recording.

**Canada:** Two-party consent required.

**EU (GDPR):** You must notify and get consent.

---

### **Best Practice: Always Ask**

Even if the law doesn't require it, always tell people you're recording:

> "Hey, do you mind if I record this? I'll send you a draft before publishing anything."

---

### **Data Privacy and Storage**

**Do:**
- ✅ Use encrypted storage (Zoom Cloud, Otter, Fireflies)
- ✅ Limit access to authorized team members
- ✅ Set a retention policy (delete after X months/years)
- ✅ Honor deletion requests (GDPR "right to erasure")

**Don't:**
- ❌ Store recordings in unprotected folders
- ❌ Share recordings publicly without consent
- ❌ Keep recordings forever "just in case"

---

### **Publishing Permission**

**Always send a draft and get approval:**

> "Hey [Name], here's the case study draft. Does this look good to you? Let me know if you'd like me to change anything."

Get written approval (email is fine) before publishing.
