# From Zoom Call to Blog Post in 30 Minutes

*A step-by-step workflow for turning any video call into published content.*

---

You just finished a Zoom call with a customer. It went great—they shared insights, told stories, and said things that would make excellent content.

Now what?

If you're like most people: nothing. The recording sits in Zoom's cloud storage, maybe forever.

But what if you could turn that call into a blog post—a real, publishable piece of content—in 30 minutes or less?

Here's exactly how to do it.

---

## The 30-Minute Workflow

| Step | Time | Action |
|------|------|--------|
| 1 | 2 min | Download transcript from Zoom |
| 2 | 5 min | Skim and highlight key moments |
| 3 | 10 min | Draft the post structure |
| 4 | 10 min | Fill in the content |
| 5 | 3 min | Polish and publish |

Let's walk through each step.

---

## Step 1: Get the Transcript (2 minutes)

If you have a paid Zoom account, transcripts are automatic.

**To download:**
1. Go to zoom.us/recording
2. Find your meeting
3. Click "Audio Transcript"
4. Download as .vtt or copy the text

**No Zoom transcript?**
- Use Otter.ai (paste the audio link or upload file)
- Use Fathom (auto-transcribes during calls)
- Use Descript (upload the video file)
- Use YouTube (upload as private video → auto-captions)

Don't have the transcript? You can still do this with notes, but it takes longer and you lose the exact quotes.

---

## Step 2: Skim and Highlight (5 minutes)

You don't need to read every word. Scan for:

**Pain quotes (their words describing problems)**
> "I was spending 4 hours every week just on..."
> "The most frustrating thing was..."
> "What kept me up at night was..."

**Result quotes (their words describing outcomes)**
> "Now I can..."
> "We went from X to Y..."
> "The biggest change has been..."

**Insight quotes (interesting observations)**
> "What surprised me was..."
> "I never expected..."
> "The thing people don't realize is..."

**Story moments (specific anecdotes)**
> "I remember this one time..."
> "The turning point was when..."
> "Let me give you an example..."

Highlight 5-10 moments. More than that is bonus, but you need at least 5 to build a post.

---

## Step 3: Draft the Structure (10 minutes)

Now decide what kind of post this will be.

**Option A: Customer Story**
- Hook with their pain
- Their journey
- What they did
- Results achieved
- Quote to close

**Option B: Problem-Solution**
- Hook with common problem (use their description)
- Why this problem exists
- How to solve it
- Their example as proof
- CTA

**Option C: Tactical How-To**
- Hook with the outcome
- Step-by-step from their experience
- Tips and lessons
- Their quote as validation
- CTA

**Option D: Insight/Observation**
- Hook with counterintuitive insight they shared
- Why most people get it wrong
- The better approach
- Evidence from their experience
- CTA

Pick the format that fits the call's best material.

---

## Step 4: Fill In the Content (10 minutes)

This is where the transcript does the heavy lifting.

**For each section of your structure:**
1. Find the relevant quote from the transcript
2. Write 1-2 sentences of context before it
3. Include the quote (use their exact words)
4. Add 1-2 sentences of commentary or transition after

**Example:**

*[Context]* When Sarah started looking for a solution, she had already tried three alternatives.

*[Quote]* "I spent six months jumping between tools, trying to make them work. Excel, then Airtable, then Notion. Nothing quite fit. I was spending more time managing the system than actually doing the work."

*[Commentary]* This is a common trap—tool-hopping without solving the underlying problem.

**Do this for each major section.** The quotes carry the content; your job is to connect them.

---

## Step 5: Polish and Publish (3 minutes)

Quick polish pass:

**Headlines:**
- Is the title compelling?
- Do subheadings make sense on skim?

**Formatting:**
- Paragraphs short (2-3 sentences)
- Quotes stand out (blockquote or bold)
- White space between sections

**Opening:**
- Does the first sentence hook?
- Would you keep reading?

**Closing:**
- Is there a CTA?
- Does it connect back to the customer's story?

**One final read.** Fix typos. Publish.

## Speed Tips

**Tip 1: Don't over-edit**

The customer's words are the content. Your job is to frame them, not rewrite them. Authentic beats polished.

**Tip 2: Let one story carry the post**

You don't need the whole call. Find one compelling thread and follow it. Better to go deep on one story than shallow on three.

**Tip 3: Use copy-paste liberally**

Direct quotes = less writing. Pull their exact words, put them in blockquotes, add context. Done.

**Tip 4: Write the headline last**

You'll know what the post is actually about once you've written it. The headline becomes obvious.

**Tip 5: Batch calls into writing sessions**

Process 3-4 calls in one sitting. You get in a flow, and suddenly you have a month of content.
